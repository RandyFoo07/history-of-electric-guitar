How It Came To Be

The first of its kind, the Rickenbacker Electro A-22, or more commonly known as the "Frying Pan", was designed to capitalize on the popularity of Hawaiian music in the 1930s.

To solve the volume problems faced by acoustic guitars, microphones were placed in front of them, only to be met by screeching sounds due to the output flowing right back into the microphone.

Early attempts at electrically amplifying the sound of strings were made on violins and banjos in the 1910s. They were however unsuccessful as the output produced was too low.

George Beauchamp was aware of the low volume that the acoustic guitar possess, and the important role it has on Hawaiian music. He produced a loud enough volume using magnetic pickups on his acoustic guitar but was disappointed by the amount of unwanted noise as an effect of it. He then met Rickenbacker, who owned a company that produced aluminium resonators and brass bodies for instruments. They designed a lap steel guitar with a solid aluminium circular body and a long neck, resembling a frying pan.
