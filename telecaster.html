How It Came To Be

With several guitar companies and the like trying to tinker with solid-body electric guitars, Leo Fender set out to create one of his own. In 1943, together with Doc Kauffman, the person who designed the tremolo arm on the Electro-Spanish guitar, they built a simple wooden guitar simply for testing pickups. The end result was a bright and clear sound similar to electric Hawaiian guitars, but without the unwanted feedback due to its solid body nature.

After several iterations of prototypes, Leo Fender ended up with a guitar that consisted of only two main pieces, the solid body of the guitar and its bolted-on neck. It was first introduced to the public as the Fender Esquire, later modified to a two-pickup model and reintroduced separately as the Broadcaster.

Legal action over the name 'Broadcaster' prompted the logo itself to be removed from the headstock of the guitars, which left only the 'Fender' logo to be seen. The term 'Nocaster' was given to specifically those guitars until Fender renamed it to the 'Telecaster' in 1951 and has stuck with it ever since.

The telecaster was one of the very few guitars that retained its initial design throughout the years, and still remained to be one of the most popular guitars now.
