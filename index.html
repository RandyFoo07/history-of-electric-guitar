The Dilemma

With the rise in popularity of orchestras and jazz ensembles in the 1920s, acoustic guitar players would soon realize the insufficient amount of volume that their instruments were producing.

This affected jazz players whom wanted the sound of single notes to be heard clearly during big band ensembles.

Thus, the demand for guitar volume amplification rose and attempts to solve the problem were made by many.

1932 - The "Frying Pan"

After countless attempts at using telephone transmitters and carbon button microphones to amplify the vibration of string instruments by placing it on their acoustic bodies, the first commercially successful attempt was made in 1932.

George Beauchamp alongside with partner Paul Barth made the first electrically amplified aluminium lap steel guitar, nicknamed the "Fying Pan".

1935 - Electro-Spanish

Due to the success of the "Frying Pan", George Beauchamp and Paul Berth worked with Adolph Rickenbacker, a large contributor to their prior project, to produce the Electro-Spanish Ken Roberts, a guitar that is made out of solid wood.

The design of the guitar was revolutionary at the time, featuring design elements that is passed down to guitars even to this current day.

1936 - ES-150

Following the footsteps of Rickenbacker, many manufacturers started to come to the scene. Gibson Guitar Corporation, a well-known acoustic company at the time, produced their first ever electric guitar - ES-150. The 'ES' stands for Electric Spanish and the '150' was the price in USD of the instrument itself.

It quickly gained popularity among jazz players in orchestras, as it was loud enough to stand out on its own. However, it still left something to be desired as there were unequal loudness across its six strings.

1948 - Telecaster

Despite Gibson's countless prototypes on what would be the first solid-body style electric guitar, they failed to make any significant impact on the market, as they themselves believe that the guitar would not succeed.

Leo Fender sought to improve on the ES-150, as it contained great volume but lacked in the tonal department, by developing a prototype of his own. Started as a crude wooden guitar to test electronic pickups, Fender soon discovered its bright and sustaining sound. After a few more revisions, it became one of the most successfully mass produced electric guitar in history.
